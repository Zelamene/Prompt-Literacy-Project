# Literature Review: Prompt Engineering, AI Literacy, and Human–AI Interaction

This section summarises selected literature that informed the development of the Prompt Literacy Project. It is not intended to be a comprehensive review, but rather an overview of research relevant to teaching students how to communicate effectively with AI systems.

## 1. Prompt Engineering

### Foundations

* **Brown et al. (2020)** – *Language Models are Few-Shot Learners*
  This study demonstrated that large language models can perform a wide range of tasks by following prompts and examples without task-specific fine-tuning. It highlighted the importance of prompt design as a key method of interacting with language models.

* **Wei et al. (2022)** – *Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning*
  The authors found that prompting models to generate intermediate reasoning steps can improve performance on certain multi-step reasoning tasks, particularly in larger language models.

### Instruction Following

* **Ouyang et al. (2022)** – *Training Language Models to Follow Instructions with Human Feedback*
  This work introduced InstructGPT, showing that models trained using human feedback generally follow user instructions more effectively. The study also noted that model performance depends on the clarity and completeness of the prompt.

### Model Limitations

* **Bang et al. (2023)** – *A Multitask, Multilingual Evaluation of ChatGPT*
  This evaluation identified limitations such as factual inaccuracies, overconfidence, and inconsistent reasoning across different tasks. The findings emphasise the importance of critically evaluating AI-generated responses.

### Summary

The prompt engineering literature suggests that the wording, structure, and level of detail in a prompt can influence the quality of an AI-generated response. These findings provide the basis for teaching structured prompting strategies such as the CLEAR framework.

## 2. AI Literacy

* **Ng et al. (2021)** – *Conceptualising AI Literacy: An Exploratory Review*
  The authors describe AI literacy as a combination of understanding, using, evaluating, and reflecting on AI technologies. Their framework highlights the importance of both practical skills and critical thinking.

* **Long & Magerko (2020)** – *What Is AI Literacy? Competencies and Design Considerations*
  This paper proposes a set of AI literacy competencies, including recognising the limitations of AI systems, evaluating AI-generated information, and understanding how AI influences decision-making.

### Summary

The AI literacy literature indicates that effective AI use involves more than technical knowledge. It also requires users to ask clear questions, interpret responses critically, and recognise the limitations of AI systems. These ideas informed the design of the CLEAR framework and the accompanying learning activities.

## 3. Human–Computer Interaction and Cognitive Load

* **Sweller (1988)** – *Cognitive Load Theory*
  Cognitive Load Theory suggests that learning can be improved by reducing unnecessary mental effort and providing structured guidance, particularly when learners are encountering new concepts.

* **Clark & Mayer (2016)** – *E-Learning and the Science of Instruction*
  The authors emphasise instructional approaches such as worked examples, guided practice, and feedback, which are widely recognised as effective strategies for supporting learning.

### Summary

These studies suggest that structured guidance and gradual skill development can support learning. The CLEAR framework was designed with these principles in mind by providing a consistent structure for writing prompts.

## 4. Educational Technology and Question Asking

* **Graesser & Person (1994)** – *Question Asking During Tutoring*
  This research found that asking meaningful questions plays an important role in learning and problem-solving. Prompt writing can be viewed as a form of question formulation, making these findings relevant to AI interactions.

* **Rosenshine (2012)** – *Principles of Instruction*
  Rosenshine identifies instructional practices such as modelling, guided practice, scaffolding, and regular feedback as effective approaches for helping students develop new skills.

### Summary

Research in education supports structured learning activities that allow students to practise, receive feedback, and refine their understanding. These principles influenced the design of the learning modules included in this project.

## 5. South African Context

The EMZAMS AI survey (2026, *n* > 80) provided context specific to South African university students. Key findings included:

* Most respondents reported using AI tools regularly.
* Common uses included understanding difficult concepts, summarising readings, and improving written work.
* Some participants reported receiving responses that were too broad, irrelevant, or inconsistent with their expectations.
* Participants also identified concerns related to academic integrity, including submitting AI-generated work without appropriate acknowledgement.

### Summary

The survey findings suggest that while AI is already widely used by many students, there are opportunities to improve prompting skills and encourage more critical and responsible use of AI tools.

## Conclusion

The literature reviewed in this section highlights three consistent themes. First, the quality of a prompt can influence the quality of an AI-generated response. Second, AI literacy includes not only using AI tools but also evaluating their outputs critically. Third, educational research supports structured guidance, practice, and feedback when developing new skills.

Together, these findings informed the development of the Prompt Literacy Project and the CLEAR framework, which aim to provide students with a practical approach to writing more effective prompts and evaluating AI-generated responses.
